Agent Banking in Bangladesh: A New Growth Sector for the Banking Industry

An econometric analysis of the top six agent banking performers in Bangladesh — identifying why BRAC Bank's lending-first model drives sustainable financial inclusion while competitors stagnate on deposit-only strategies.

✍ MD. Nazmus Sakib 📅 March 2020 ⏱ 12 min read 🎓 MBA Thesis, IBA — University of Dhaka
Agent Banking Bangladesh Financial Inclusion Fintech Banking

Abstract

Agent banking has emerged as one of the most promising instruments for financial inclusion in Bangladesh, extending formal banking services to the unbanked population — particularly in rural and semi-urban areas where branch infrastructure is economically unviable. This paper analyses the operational models, growth trajectories, and sustainability metrics of the top six agent banking performers in Bangladesh between 2017 and 2022, with a focus on understanding what differentiates high-performing institutions from the rest.

Through econometric analysis of Bangladesh Bank data and annual reports, I find that BRAC Bank's lending-first model — providing credit through agent channels rather than restricting agents to deposit and payment functions — is the primary differentiator for long-term, sustainable growth and genuine financial inclusion impact.

Background: Agent Banking in Bangladesh

Bangladesh Bank issued the Agent Banking Guidelines in 2013, allowing scheduled banks to deliver limited banking services through third-party agents — typically mobile phone retailers, pharmacies, or small shop owners — equipped with point-of-sale devices and biometric authentication.

The policy was motivated by a structural gap: as of 2013, only 31% of Bangladeshi adults held a formal bank account, with rural penetration below 20%. Physical branch expansion was cost-prohibitive; the average cost of operating a rural branch exceeded BDT 5 million per year, with most rural branches remaining loss-making for 3–5 years post-opening.

Agent banking offered a lower-cost alternative: agents operate on a commission basis, requiring no fixed staff, no owned premises, and minimal technology infrastructure beyond a mobile device and reliable internet connectivity.

"The average agent banking outlet costs a bank approximately BDT 18,000 per year to maintain — compared to BDT 5 million for a rural branch. The economics are not marginal; they are transformative."

Industry Growth: 2017–2024

The sector has experienced compounding growth since 2017. From approximately 6,400 active agent outlets nationwide, the network has expanded to over 28,000 by 2024 — a 4.4x increase in seven years. Transaction values have grown even faster, reflecting both network expansion and deepening usage per outlet.

Agent Banking Outlets — Industry Growth (2017–2024)

The growth has not been linear. A notable acceleration occurred in 2020–2021, driven by COVID-19 pandemic effects: branch closures, mobility restrictions, and government social protection payments disbursed digitally through agent banking channels created a structural step-change in adoption and trust.

Transaction Volume Trends

Raw outlet count understates the sector's true trajectory. Transaction volumes — measured in total BDT value processed — have grown at a compound annual rate of approximately 45% between 2017 and 2024, reflecting both new user acquisition and rising transaction frequency among existing users.

Total Transaction Volume — BDT Billion (2017–2024)

The gap between outlet growth (4.4x) and transaction value growth (13.8x) indicates that average revenue per outlet has increased by approximately 3x — a signal of maturing usage behaviour, not just network expansion. Users are shifting from occasional to regular use, and the transaction mix is moving towards higher-value payments such as remittances and salary disbursement.

Bank-by-Bank Comparison: Top 6 Performers

Among the six banks with the largest agent banking networks as of 2022, BRAC Bank maintained a clear leadership position both in absolute outlet count and in growth rate. The following chart compares 2020 and 2022 outlet counts for the top six, revealing both the scale of BRAC Bank's dominance and the divergence in growth rates.

Two patterns stand out. First, BRAC Bank's outlet count grew by 81% over the period — significantly faster than any competitor. Second, the gap between BRAC Bank and Dutch-Bangla Bank (the second-largest network) widened substantially, from approximately 400 outlets in 2020 to over 1,600 in 2022.

The Lending-First Model: Key Findings

The central research question was: what explains BRAC Bank's sustained outperformance? The econometric analysis points to a structural difference in business model design rather than operational or technological advantages.

Deposit-Only vs. Lending-First

Most agent banking operators in Bangladesh use agents as deposit-taking and payment-processing nodes. Customers open accounts, receive salary payments, and send/receive remittances through agents — but credit access requires a visit to a physical branch.

BRAC Bank, by contrast, extended its microfinance lending capabilities through agent banking channels from an early stage. Agents can initiate loan applications, conduct preliminary KYC, and serve as loan repayment collection points. This creates a fundamentally different value proposition: customers are not just stored-value accounts, but active credit customers with deeper bank relationships.

Why Lending-First Drives Sustainable Inclusion

  • Higher customer retention: Credit customers have a functional reason to maintain an active account. Deposit-only customers frequently become dormant within 6–12 months of account opening.
  • Recurring transaction volumes: Loan disbursements and repayments generate predictable, recurring transaction flows that sustain agent commission income — creating economic incentive for agent retention.
  • Deeper financial inclusion: Access to credit, not just a deposit account, is the primary mechanism through which the financially excluded household can smooth consumption, invest in productive assets, and build credit history. Deposit-only models create accounts; lending-first models create financial inclusion.
  • Agent loyalty: Agents earning commission on loan origination and repayment collection have materially higher incomes than agents limited to deposit/payment services, reducing churn and improving service quality.
The deposit-only model treats agent banking as a distribution channel. The lending-first model treats it as a relationship-building infrastructure. The data shows that only the latter produces sustainable, compounding growth.

Conclusion & Implications

Bangladesh's agent banking sector has demonstrated that the infrastructure for mass financial inclusion exists and is scalable. The sector's 7-year growth trajectory is among the fastest of any comparable market globally. However, the data reveals a critical bifurcation: institutions that confine agent banking to deposit and payment functions are growing more slowly and building shallower customer relationships than those that extend credit through the same channels.

For policymakers at Bangladesh Bank, the implication is clear: regulatory frameworks that restrict agent banking to passive financial services — while understandable from a risk management perspective — constrain the sector's potential to deliver genuine financial inclusion at scale. A phased, risk-tiered approach to agent-originated credit, with appropriate safeguards, would accelerate inclusion without materially increasing systemic risk.

For banking practitioners, the strategic lesson is that agent banking viewed as a cost-reduction exercise will produce modest, easily-competed-away gains. Agent banking viewed as a new channel for relationship banking — capable of initiating, deepening, and retaining credit relationships with previously unbanked households — has the potential to reshape Bangladesh's retail banking landscape over the next decade.
